What Is “Adimu and You”?

An Iyawo lesson in offering and self-relationship

Adimu as Offering

Adimu is a sacred offering set before divine power, ancestral force, or spiritual principle. It may include food, drink, fruit, grains, or other appropriate items according to context, instruction, and tradition.

Lesson Insight: An offering is not random. It must be intentional, respectful, and rightly placed.

Adimu as Mirror

For the Iyawo, Adimu also becomes a mirror. How you prepare it, how you approach it, and how you carry yourself around it reveal something about your inner condition. Disorder in the process often reflects disorder in the self.

Lesson Insight: The offering is outward, but the lesson is inward.

The 3 IoWLH Pillars Applied to Adimu

How the Iyawo is shaped through the practice

1) Know Thyself

Awareness Intention Reflection

Before preparing Adimu, the Iyawo must ask: What spirit am I bringing to this work? Am I rushed, careless, distracted, resentful, thankful, sincere, or disciplined? Adimu begins with self-observation.

  • Check your internal state before beginning
  • Know whether you are acting from duty or devotion
  • Notice where your mind is during preparation

2) Cleanse and Purify

Cleanliness Order Preparation

Adimu should not be approached casually. The body, the hands, the space, and the materials should reflect respect. Cleanliness is not merely physical; it also includes intention, speech, and manner.

  • Clean the space before arranging the offering
  • Use proper care in handling the items
  • Remove unnecessary confusion and clutter

3) Living in Alignment With Absolute Truth

Truth Integrity Right Relationship

Adimu teaches honesty. You cannot offer truthfully while living carelessly. The Iyawo is asked to bring actions into harmony with what is being declared spiritually. The offering should support truth, not substitute for it.

  • Let the offering match a truthful life effort
  • Do not use ritual to cover indiscipline
  • Approach the work with sincerity and alignment

The 2 IoWLH Principles Within This Lesson

How the Iyawo understands the work

We Seek No Followers

The Iyawo is not being trained to imitate mechanically. This lesson is meant to cultivate understanding, discipline, and self-responsibility. Adimu is not about performance for approval. It is about right relationship.

Many Paths, 1 Truth

Different houses and traditions may arrange, name, or emphasize offerings differently. Yet truth remains: the offering should reflect reverence, order, sincerity, and alignment. Forms vary. Truth does not.

Iyawo Lesson Flow

A simple sequence for “Adimu and You”
Step 1

Pause

  • Become still
  • Check your mindset
  • Remember who and what the work is for
Step 2

Prepare

  • Clean the space
  • Gather the materials properly
  • Handle the items with respect
Step 3

Offer

  • Place the Adimu deliberately
  • Speak clearly and truthfully
  • Remain present, not mechanical
Step 4

Reflect

  • What did the process show about you?
  • Were you ordered, clean, and sincere?
  • What needs strengthening?

Core Teaching

The Iyawo must learn that Adimu is not just about giving something. It is about becoming someone: more orderly, more conscious, more truthful, and more aligned in sacred relationship.

Adimu = Offering + Discipline + Self-Revelation
